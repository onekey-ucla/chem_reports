Organic Chemistry Video Pilot

Responses are separated by class. MQ (Section A) includes 171 students and DD (Section B / CHEM14BE) includes 210; this report compares them side-by-side.

Anonymized LMS exports. Numbers in the question-breakdown bars use each cohort’s full roster (blanks shown). Keyed-rate percentages exclude students who skipped that stem.

Watch time

Most students reported watching the full video.

83.6% of MQ and 79.0% of DD reported watching the entire video; only 1.2% and 2.4% respectively said they did not watch. The two sections overall showed very similar patterns.

Chemistry content knowledge

Students answered most chemistry content questions correctly.

The question about implicit carbons is the only item clearly below the 90% benchmark in both sections, at 82.6% (MQ) and 83.1% (DD): the “where the bond connects” distractor pulled about one in eight students.

Both sections perform within about two percentage points on every stem; section-level differences are small. Stem 3D (implicit carbons) is the clearest candidate for whole-class debrief.

Pre-post video responses

After the video, students reported more connection and excitement and less nervousness. Real-world connection and excitement increased in both sections, while nervousness moved toward neutral. Note these are simple pre/post mean differences, not a paired test.

Post-video feedback questions

Students generally found the immersive format helpful (see means below); other post-only items capture novelty, rewatches, and whether the video shifted how students think about ochem.

Post qualitative responses

One item asked what stood out about the video and whether it changed how students think about organic chemistry. 369 students submitted non-empty text (MQ 165 · DD 204; 12 left the essay field blank). The summaries below use reflexive thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke’s six-phase framework; overview in Ahmed et al., 2025, DOI 10.1016/j.glmedi.2025.100198). Themes are constructed through interpretation, not “discovered” as neutral facts. Where noted, keyword counts are supplementary sensitizing aids only (overlap across themes is common).

Open-ended responses — thematic summary

Theme 1 — Everyday anchoring through coffee (and embodied experience)

Students attach meaning when organic chemistry is tethered to familiar experience—especially coffee—or to smell, taste, receptor physiology, or daily rituals so that abstract structures feel concrete.

  • Coffee / café narrative as a pedagogical hinge.
  • Sensory physiology (olfaction, gustation, receptors, caffeine / adenosine) as evidence of relevance.

Illustrative keyword family: ~132 / 369 responses (overlap allowed).

Example verbatim extract
[MQ 4] One thing that stood out to me in the video was how organic molecules in coffee are responsible for smell, taste, and stimulant effects. It was interesting because when I think of ochem, I really only think about molecule diagrams and symbols, so it was particularly interesting to see the content be applied to real-life scenarios and how it is responsible for things that may affect us in our lives.

Theme 2 — “Making it manageable”: clarity, pace, digestibility

Students value plain language, brevity, staged difficulty, and the sense that foundational ideas are within reach.

Illustrative keyword family: ~81 / 369 (overlap allowed).

Example verbatim extract
[DD 12] What stood out to me most about this video is how they made it really simple and straightforward, making the concepts easy to understand. This changed how I thought about organic chemistry because it made it less intimidating.

Theme 3 — Multimodality & immersion (seeing carbon in space)

Animations, 3D representations, wedges/hashes, moving diagrams, and the café “set” are cited as scaffolds for both understanding and motivation.

Illustrative keyword family: ~93 / 369 (overlap allowed).

Example verbatim extract
[DD 46] The animations stood out the most to me since they made it easier to understand the concepts.

Theme 4 — Affective labor: fear, stigma, and emotional repositioning

Students name anticipatory anxiety, peer horror stories, and instructor “weeder” talk, and describe whether the video soothes, partially helps, or fails to move that anxiety.

  • Campus / peer narratives vs. a gentler counter-narrative.
  • Critique that fear-mongering from faculty compounds worry (e.g. [DD 24]).
  • Paradoxical effect: an anti-fear video can signal that the course must be frightening (e.g. [DD 171]).

Illustrative keyword family: ~92 / 369 (overlap allowed).

Example verbatim extract
[DD 5] Nothing stood out to me and nothing changed how I think about organic chemistry. I've just heard from all around me how difficult and scary and grade tanking it is that this video is not going to magically make me feel comfortable about it.

Theme 5 — Conceptual recalibration & prior knowledge

Students articulate new understanding of what counts as organic chemistry (often carbon-centered), skeletal / shorthand notation, or links back to general chemistry—sometimes surprised that the course is more visual than expected.

Illustrative keyword family: ~118 / 369 (overlap allowed).

Example verbatim extract
[MQ 105] I had always thought that organic chemistry was about the human body and or chemistry happening in plants and animals. I had no idea that the definition of organic chemistry was just the chemistry of compounds with carbon inside of them.

Theme 6 — Limits of impact: skepticism, non-conversion, friction

Non-shifts in thinking, pushback on “softening” difficulty, preference for utility framing (e.g. exams, MCAT), disinterest in applied examples, or trouble locating the assigned video.

Illustrative keyword family: ~30 / 369 — undercounts mildly skeptical wording (overlap allowed).

Example verbatim extract
[DD 151] Nothing really stood out to me about this video. I honestly think trying to make students think the chemistry series here isnt hard is just going to trick them. The chem series is hard, the department doesnt need to lie. Maybe make organic chemistry more appealing by focusing more on why taking the class is useful, such as the MCAT or real word scenarios.

Cross-cutting observations

  1. Co-occurrence is typical. Many responses weave real-world examples, praise for visuals, and emotional relief in the same short paragraph; themes are not mutually exclusive boxes.
  2. MQ vs. DD. No statistical test of theme prevalence was run. Close reading did not show two radically different “cultures”; differences are subtle (e.g. DD with somewhat more explicit coffee mentions in this slice).
  3. Practice-facing implications (descriptive, not causal):
    • Strength signal: For many students, contextualization + spatial representations + tone read as engaging and legible.
    • Risk signal: A credibility gap can open if in-class messaging later contradicts the pilot’s reassurance—see also [DD 24], [DD 78] (“more difficult than they painted it”).
    • Access: Sparse but important notes that the video was hard to find in the LMS argue for navigation / placement checks.
