# Thematic Analysis: Open-Ended Responses to the Organic Chemistry Video Pilot

**Corpus:** LMS export item *“What stood out to you most about this video, and did it change how you think about organic chemistry in any way?”*  

- **MQ (Section A):** 165 substantive responses (6 blank in source file)  
- **DD (CHEM14BE):** 204 substantive responses (6 blank)  
- **Total coded units:** *N* = 369 text responses (HTML stripped; submissions remain de-identified at roster level)

**Analytic approach:** Reflexive thematic analysis following Braun & Clarke’s six-phase process, as summarized in Ahmed *et al.* (2025) *Globalization and Health* ([DOI 10.1016/j.glmedi.2025.100198](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.glmedi.2025.100198))—familiarization → initial coding → searching for themes → reviewing themes → defining/naming → reporting. Themes are **constructed through interpretation**, not discovered as neutral facts; labels condense patterns judged most salient for instructor-facing insight.

**Reflexivity:** This pass was produced in a single analytic cycle with systematic re-reading of the full *N* = 369 file, memoing, and consolidation. A human research team would typically split coding, hold consensus meetings, and log an audit trail; here, limits are explicit: one analyst session, English text only, no member checking with students. Frequencies below that use **keyword families** are **illustrative sensitizing aids only** (responses can fit multiple themes; many nuanced posts do not trigger a crude string match). They must not be read as latent-class percentages.

---

## Phase 1 — Familiarization

Repeated reading showed students repeatedly looping through a small set of *storylines*: (i) praise for **coffee / everyday instantiation** of ochem; (ii) praise for **clear, slow, simplified** exposition; (iii) strong emphasis on **visuals, animations, wedges/hashes**, and **immersion / café mise-en-scène**; (iv) explicit talk of **fear, intimidation, and campus lore** (“weeder,” “terrifying”) juxtaposed with **reassurance**; (v) substantive **conceptual pickups** (carbon-centric definition, skeletal notation, receptors); (vi) **non-conversion narratives**—“interesting but didn’t change how I think,” logistics, or pushback that the video glosses difficulty.

Early memos also noted tonal range: exuberant optimism, pragmatic appreciation, cynical dismissal, and ambivalence (“less nervous **but still**…”).

## Phase 3 & 4 — Candidate themes → review

Candidate clusters were tested for **internal coherence** (do extracts tell one story?) and **external distinction** (does the cluster blur into another?). During review, “presenter tone” was grouped with multimodal immersion where it co-occurred with café setting and pacing; wedges/hashes remained under spatial representation rather than a separate niche theme because of heavy overlap with Theme 3.

### Theme 1 — Everyday anchoring through coffee (and embodied experience)

**Definition:** Students invest meaning when abstract ochem is **tethered to a familiar artifact**—chiefly coffee—or to smell, taste, stimulant physiology, or daily rituals. This theme is interpretive linkage: carbon structures become *felt* phenomena.

**Sub-themes:**

1. Coffee / café narrative as pedagogical hinge.
2. Sensory physiology (olfaction, gustation, receptors, caffeine/adenosine) as evidence of relevance.

**Full verbatim extracts**

**[MQ 4]**

> One thing that stood out to me in the video was how organic molecules in coffee are responsible for smell, taste, and stimulant effects. It was interesting because when I think of ochem, I really only think about molecule diagrams and symbols, so it was particularly interesting to see the content be applied to real-life scenarios and how it is responsible for things that may affect us in our lives.

**[DD 38]**

> I liked the real life application of the explanations. For instance, how the structure of molecules in coffee or sucralose relate to how our olfactory receptors can receive or not receive information about the smell. The connections of organic chemistry to real world experiences made me more excited to learn organic chemistry, which has been thought of as an abstract thing isolated from application.

**[DD 112]**

> Caffeine is absorbed into out bodies into our brain, the brain grabs adenosine, since caffeine gets into the adenosine receptors we feel stimulated instead of calm. Yes, it made me realize organic chemistry is more applicable to the real world than I thought.

**[DD 26]**

> I think coffee and chemistry is a really cool concept, especially as someone who worked as a barista in high school, it was cool to see this connection

**Illustrative coding note:** Responses may blend Theme 1 with Themes 3–5 in the same paragraph; the excerpt above is assigned here for **real-world/sensory anchoring**.

**Indicative keyword prevalence (overlap allowed):** 132 / 369 posts hit coffee / everyday / receptors / caffeine family (supplementary **only**).

### Theme 2 — “Making it manageable”: clarity, pace, digestibility

**Definition:** Praise for linguistic and structural **economy**—plain language, brevity, staged difficulty, reassurance that foundational pieces are attainable.

**Full verbatim extracts**

> What stood out to me most about this video is how they made it really simple and straightforward, making the concepts easy to understand. This changed how I thought about organic chemistry because it made it less intimidating.

**[DD 103]**

**[DD 44]** *(also touches confidence and modality; primary assignment here for clarity-vs-textbook contrast)*

> What stood out to me in this video was the simplicity of explaining where carbons are located, what slashes or wedges represent, etc., compared to a textbook that is generally dense and heavily worded. It did not change how i think about ochem. I feel pretty neautral about taking it. I'm not scared but I'm definitely not confident.

**[MQ 146]** *(overlap with Theme 4—reassurance)*

> The part that stood out to me most was that organic chemistry is the study of carbon-based life forms. I thought it would be much more complicated than that.

### Theme 3 — Multimodality & immersion — seeing carbon in space

**Definition:** Animated or static **spatial representations** (3D models, moving diagrams, wedges/hashes) plus **environmental immersion** (café set, “YouTube-like” energy) cited as cognitive and motivational scaffolds.

**Full verbatim extracts**

**[MQ 3]** *(immersion + clarity + café setting)*

> This was such an exciting way to be introduced to organic chemistry! I would say the combination of the immersion and minimalist formats made this video so much more engaging. Instead of the presenters talking to the audience as if they are simply, well an audience, the cafe setting gives the audience (or at least me) the perception that I am at the cafe, increasing my focus. I also appreciate that the concepts were presented in a straightforward way (no overwhelming/dense content, easy to understand language, enthusiatic tone, etc.). While I know organic chemistry won't always be this exciting, it does make me wish there was a video like this for every topic!

**[MQ 6]** *(3D / wedges–hashes; overlap with conceptual theme)*

> What stood out to me the most about this video was the complexity and 3D nature of organic chemistry via wedges and hashes, in which it helped me realize that molecules aren't simply flat drawings on a page but instead represent physical objects with distinct, specific shapes and conformations that enable the formation of such incredibly intricate carbon-based structures in the natural world. It offered greater excitement for the implications of honing one's understanding of the greater operations of life and carbon-based structures through a strong. foundation in organic chemistry.

**[DD 46]**

> The animations stood out the most to me since they made it easier to understand the concepts.

**Keyword family (supplementary):** 93 / 369.

### Theme 4 — Affective labor: fear, stigma, and emotional repositioning

**Definition:** Students name **anticipatory anxiety** (“weeder,” horror stories) and evaluate whether the video **reframes self-efficacy**. Includes both successful soothing and **failed** or **partial** modulation.

**Sub-themes**

1. Campus / peer horror narratives vs gentle counter-narrative.
2. Critique of **instructor fear-mongering** as compounding anxiety (*[DD 24]*).
3. **Paradoxical reassurance backfire:** a dedicated anti-fear artifact can inadvertently confirm danger (*[DD 171]*).

**Full verbatim extracts**

**[DD 5]**

> Nothing stood out to me and nothing changed how I think about organic chemistry. I've just heard from all around me how difficult and scary and grade tanking it is that this video is not going to magically make me feel comfortable about it.

**[DD 7]** *(partial relief—paired with horror-story framing)*

> This video stood out to me because at first I was very scared to take organic chemistry but now I feel like its a course that is not going to be as bad as it is made out to be.

**[MQ 70]** *(design choice: naming anxiety openly)*

> I like how the video addressed the typical anxieties people have about taking organic chemistry, because it made me feel like the video creators actually understood and cared about students. Hearing them acknowledge people's worries about the difficulty of organic chemistry made me feel less nervous about taking the course.

**[DD 24]** *(critique of instructor “weeder” discourse)*

> I liked how easily it broke down the content. I also feel like the negative attitude that professors in ochem may have, such as beginning the course by saying this will be a weeder course and most of you will struggle makes it challenging to feel like one has the possibility to succeed.

**[DD 143]**

> What stood out to me the most about the video is the perspective it placed on me about organic chemistry. I have always been scared about taking the class because I always hear negative things about it and how hard it is, but this has made it a little more bearable to look forward to.

**[DD 171]** *(paradoxical reassurance)*

> I didn't know the actual definition of what organic chemistry was before watching the video, so that stuck out to me, but all of the other concepts in the video I was already familiar with from previous biology and chemistry courses I've taken. Also, I know it was probably supposed to make me feel less nervous about taking organic chemistry, but honestly, the fact that a video needed to be made specifically to tell us not to be afraid of organic chemistry reinforced to me that I definitely need to be afraid of taking organic chemistry... It was a good video though, and clearly explained what organic chemistry is and how it relates to the real world.

**Keyword family (supplementary):** 92 / 369.

### Theme 5 — Conceptual recalibration & integration with prior knowledge

**Definition:** Students articulate **new explicit knowledge** (carbon-centered scope, skeletal shorthand, implicit hydrogens) or **bridge** from gen chem / 14-series. Some note surprise that ochem is **less algebra-forward** or more **visual-topological**.

**Full verbatim extracts**

**[DD 30]** *(unexpected scope—“carbon” vs “shapes only”)*

> I didn't realize that organic chemistry was about carbon specifically (I originally just thought it was about the shapes of molecules). I think the video made the main concepts of organic chemistry seem a lot simpler than I had originally thought it to be, which is reassuring.

**[MQ 105]** *(revising prior definition linked to biology/plants narrative)*

> I had always thought that organic chemistry was about the human body and or chemistry happening in plants and animals. I had no idea that the definition of organic chemistry was just the chemistry of compounds with carbon inside of them.

**[DD 28]** *(wedges/hashes → 3D literacy)*

> What stood out to me the most was what wedges and hashes tell us and how they indicate their 3D dimensional structures, which I didn't know before.

**Keyword family (supplementary):** 118 / 369.

## Cross-cutting analytic observations

1. **Co-occurrence is the norm:** Many responses braid Themes 1 + 3 + 4 in a single sentence (coffee *and* visuals *and* emotional relief). Thematic analysis here privileges **interpretive story** over forced mutual exclusivity.
2. **MQ vs DD:** No formal statistical test was run on theme prevalence; side-by-side reading did **not** reveal a stark divergent second culture—differences are subtle (DD slightly more explicit coffee mentions in this corpus slice; MQ contains some of the longest enthusiast monologues). A future **deductive codebook** with dual coders could quantify.
3. **Pedagogical implication map (practice-facing, not causal):**
  - Strength signal: **contextualization + spatial visualization + tone** co-produce engagement *for many*.  
  - Risk signal: **credibility gap** if downstream course tone contradicts pilot reassurance (*[DD 24]*, *[DD 78]*).  
  - Access & equity: **findability** comments suggest navigation / LMS placement merit audit.
